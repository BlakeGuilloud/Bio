A Brief History on Guilloud

This uncommon surname is of Old French origin, and is one of the varied group of derivative names generated by the male personal name "Guillaume", the Old French form of an ancient Germanic given name composed of the elements "wil", will, desire, with "helm", helmet, protection. Guillon is a double diminutive of "Guillaume", being a diminutive form of "Guille", which is itself the usual diminutive form of "Guillaume". This name and its many variant forms were introduced into England after the Norman Conquest of 1066, and the earliest recordings of the derivative surnames are usually found in England, as in the example below. French forms of surnames from diminutives of "Guille" include Guillet(on), Guillot, Guillotin, Guillon (eau), Guillou(x), and Guillond. Recordings of the name from French Church Registers include: the christening of Catherine, daughter of Etienne Guillon, at Angers, Maine-et-Loire, in December 1643, and the marriage of Francois Guillon and Perrine Baudry on July 7th 1692, at Andreze, Maine-et-Loire. The name was re-introduced into England by Huguenot refugees; one Francoise Guillon was christened at Castle Street French Huguenot Church, London, on October 13th 1695. The first recorded spelling of the family name is shown to be that of Peter Gillame, which was dated 1276, in the "Calendar of Letter Books of the City of London", during the reign of King Edward 1, known as "The Hammer of the Scots", 1272 - 1307. Surnames became necessary when governments introduced personal taxation. In England this was known as Poll Tax. Throughout the centuries, surnames in every country have continued to "develop" often leading to astonishing variants of the original spelling.

A Brief History on Ghengis Khan

In 1211, after the conquest of Western Xia, Genghis Khan planned again to conquer the Jin dynasty. The commander of the Jin dynasty army made a tactical mistake in not attacking the Mongols at the first opportunity. Instead, the Jin commander sent a messenger, Ming-Tan, to the Mongol side, who defected and told the Mongols that the Jin army was waiting on the other side of the pass. At this engagement fought at Badger Pass the Mongols massacred hundreds of thousands of Jin troops. In 1215 Genghis besieged, captured, and sacked the Jin capital of Zhongdu (modern-day Beijing). This forced the Emperor Xuanzong to move his capital south to Kaifeng, abandoning the northern half of his kingdom to the Mongols.
